Dissertation journey

From fragmented interests to a research question: tracing the full thought process through brainstorming, exploring, narrowing, and landing.

Part 1 — Where it started

The problem with the original topic

The original dissertation proposed studying Home Office and Prime Ministerial discourse on securitization vs. humanitarianism in UK asylum governance (2015-2025) — tracing how gendered narratives of vulnerability, threat, and deservingness are mobilized to legitimize exclusion.

Three problems forced a change: (1) Home Office documents deliberately use depoliticised language that hides the hierarchies of deservingness. (2) A desire for future-oriented research rather than pure critique. (3) Compassion fatigue — the humanitarian sector crumbling, UNHCR offices closing during the 2025 internship, and the emotional weight of studying suffering daily.

The interests that survived — in your own words

"Reclaiming mobility as a right"

"Securitisation"

"The benefits and disadvantages of how corporate partnerships reshape humanitarian practice"

"Study the system itself as a site of negotiation between humanitarian principles and market/state logic"

"A system where who gets to move and how they're governed once displaced are both being reshaped by the same convergence of state security interests and corporate capital"

"The politics, the far right, nationalism, the ideas of the nation-state and how they define the community"

And beneath all of it: gender and feminism, race and postcolonialism, the desire for something constructive

08The critical question: which feminism structures governance?key turn
Feminism isn't one thing. The version adopted determines what's visible — and who gets protection.
Liberal feminism: "Add women and stir." Gender-sensitive asylum guidance, vulnerability categories, procedural adjustments. Operates within the existing architecture without questioning its racial, colonial, or class-based structure. This is what governments mean by "feminist" credentials on migration.
Nationalist feminism: Weaponizing gender to justify exclusion. Migrant men as threats to "our" women. Sexual violence narratives legitimizing border closure. This is what far-right parties do with gender.
Intersectional / decolonial feminism: The border regime itself is a feminist issue — structured by race, coloniality, class, citizenship (Achiume, Mohanty, Freedman, Krause, Crenshaw). The refugee definition historically protected the European intellectual man. Gendered human rights violations systemically under-recognised.
The analytical engine: Liberal feminism can't counter nationalist feminism because it doesn't address race and coloniality. The contest isn't progressive vs. regressive — it's between fundamentally different feminisms. The version adopted has material consequences for who moves and who's protected.

One possible theoretical architecture emerged — but it's not yet settled. The idea: migration governance is the empirical focus, and the "which feminism" question is the theoretical engine.
The idea: Different feminisms (liberal, nationalist, intersectional) structure governance differently. The version a society operates under determines what it can see, who it protects, and whose mobility it enables. You'd study a specific site in UK migration governance where all three are visible and in tension.
The argument it would make: The contest over migration governance isn't simply progressive vs. regressive — it's between fundamentally different feminisms. Liberal feminism can't counter nationalist feminism because both share a blind spot on race and coloniality. Part 5 — Empirical cases currently being explored

Neither the theoretical framework nor the empirical case is fully settled. Each option below was introduced for specific reasons and has distinct strengths and weaknesses. A decision needs to be made with the supervisor about which best serves the argument within practical constraints.

Case 1: UK suspension of refugee family reunion (strongest current option)

What it is: In September 2025, Labour suspended the refugee family reunion route — the primary safe, legal pathway for women and children to reach family members with refugee status in the UK. 92% of beneficiaries were women and children. The replacement requires £29,000 income, English language tests, and fees of nearly £3,000.

Why it was introduced: Gender emerges naturally (92% women and children — not imposed). Nationalism is the driver (Reform UK pressure, "unfairness" framing). All three feminisms are visible: Labour's liberal feminism (claiming compassion while closing the route), Reform UK's nationalist feminism (protecting British communities from "burden"), intersectional feminist civil society contesting both (60+ organisations, Women for Refugee Women, UNHCR, Helen Bamber Foundation).

The community/society dimension: The government justified the suspension by claiming refugee families burden communities. Civil society argues family reunion builds communities — reunited families integrate faster, form social connections, contribute economically. The case is fundamentally a contest over what "community" means: who belongs, on what terms, and who decides. This connects to the bigger question about democracy and the kind of society migration governance reveals.

The capitalism dimension: Not a named corporate actor, but the marketisation of a humanitarian right. Family unity restructured from a rights-based entitlement into an economic transaction — you earn your family by proving market value (£29,000). The housing market and privatised asylum accommodation (Serco/Mears/Clearsprings) are the infrastructure failure the government uses to justify the restriction.

Data available: Home Office family reunion statistics disaggregated by sex, nationality, and age. Hansard parliamentary debates. NGO reports and advocacy materials. UNHCR observations. Migration Observatory analysis. Germany and Austria comparisons. All in English.

Open question: Is the corporate/partnership element strong enough, or does it need supplementing?

Case 2: Immigration detention of women

Why introduced: Gender is built into the case by design — women-specific facilities (Yarl's Wood, Derwentside) run by Serco on contracts worth £69.5 million. 85% of detained women are GBV survivors. 89% are ultimately released (shouldn't have been detained). HMIP inspection reports are gendered, detailed, and public. Derwentside converted from female to male in 2024 to serve deterrence goals. Strongest option for the corporate/partnership dimension — Serco is the named actor whose profit incentives directly shape gendered outcomes. Staff trained to call women "prisoners." What does it reveal about liberal democracy that a state outsources the detention of traumatised women to a corporation?

Open question: Is this too focused on critique of suffering? Does it connect strongly enough to community and society?

Case 3: The privatised asylum accommodation system

Why introduced: Serco, Mears, and Clearsprings run the entire asylum accommodation system under contracts that have ballooned from £4.5 billion to £15.3 billion. Hotels more profitable than dispersal housing. Performance penalties less than 1% of revenue. Subcontractor lists five years out of date. Strongest on the capitalism/corporate partnership dimension. Gender enters as one dimension but isn't the central organising principle — the system isn't inherently gendered.

Why partially stepped back: Bolting gender onto this system required assumptions that didn't hold (GBV assumption, lack of sex-disaggregated dispersal data, GIS mapping promising more than the data could deliver). Still relevant as context or supporting evidence within another case.

Part 5b — How the empirical exploration evolved

What was tested and why it was set aside

The dispersal system as a gendered implementation gap: Tried to map the gap between gender-sensitive policy commitments and protection outcomes through GIS spatial analysis. Three problems: (1) the system isn't inherently gendered — gender was imposed, (2) the framing assumed all women face GBV, (3) sex-disaggregated dispersal data doesn't exist so GIS couldn't deliver what it promised.

Feminist foreign policy as the policy object: FFP opened the "which feminism" intersection (feminist governance + migration + nationalism) but is being abolished across countries (Sweden 2022, Germany 2025). Too narrow as a disappearing object — but the analytical insight it produced (competing feminisms) became the theoretical framework.

The EU Pact on Migration and Asylum: Securitization dressed as management. Closest fit for the capitalism-nationalism-humanitarianism triangle but risked returning to critique-of-suffering territory and required non-English primary sources for depth.

Italy (Meloni, femonationalism, Albania model): Holds all six threads in extreme form — first female PM weaponising feminism against migrants, strongest solidarity cities in Europe, offshore detention. But primary sources are in Italian. Fascinating case for someone based in Italy; impractical from London in four months.

Part 5c — Key finding: the UK's gender-sensitive commitments are thinner than they appear

What exists: Home Office "Gender Issues in the Asylum Claim" guidance (procedural — you can request a female interviewer). Istanbul Convention and CEDAW obligations. Bespoke guidance on FGM, trafficking, sexual orientation.

What doesn't exist: No structural gender design in the asylum system. Gender sensitivity concentrated in decision-making, almost entirely absent from accommodation, dispersal, detention, and support. No gender-specific dispersal policy. No gender requirements in AASC contracts. No sex-disaggregated dispersal data in reportable format — the Home Office can't monitor its own commitments.

Why this matters: The thinness of the commitment is itself analytically interesting. It reveals liberal feminism in practice: procedural gestures within a system whose structure is untouched by gender analysis. The "which feminism" framework explains why — liberal feminism adds women to the existing architecture without questioning the architecture itself.

Part 5d — What this process has established

What is certain

The country: UK. Practical advantages (English, London, access, knowledge) outweigh the pull of other cases.

The bigger question: What does migration governance reveal about what liberal democracies actually value? This holds regardless of which framework or case is chosen.

The analytical disposition: Curiosity about how negotiation works. Interested in the system, the people affected, and the people contesting — less interested in studying the powerful directly. Wants to understand the mechanism, not just expose the outcome.

What's not settled: Whether the "which feminism" debate should drive the argument or be one lens among several. The specific empirical case. The methodology. The exact research question. These need the supervisor conversation.

The six threads as questions about society

Nationalism asks: who is the political community? Who belongs and who is excluded?

Gender asks: whose bodies are governed, protected, or abandoned — and on whose terms?

Capitalism asks: who profits from the system of inclusion and exclusion?

Mobility asks: is freedom of movement a right or a privilege distributed by race, class, and nationality?

Solidarity asks: when the state fails its own principles, what emerges from below?

Power dynamics asks: who decides all of the above, and how is that decision legitimised?

Migration governance is where all of these become concrete — where abstract commitments to equality and democracy are tested against reality.
