Part 1 — Where it started

The problem with the original topic

The original dissertation proposed studying Home Office and Prime Ministerial discourse on securitization vs. humanitarianism in UK asylum governance (2015-2025) — tracing how gendered narratives of vulnerability, threat, and deservingness are mobilized to legitimize exclusion.

Three problems forced a change: (1) Home Office documents deliberately use depoliticised language that hides the hierarchies of deservingness. (2) A desire for future-oriented research rather than pure critique. (3) Compassion fatigue — the humanitarian sector crumbling, UNHCR offices closing during the 2025 internship, and the emotional weight of studying suffering daily.

↓ "which feminism?" — the question that sharpened everything
08The critical question: which feminism structures governance?key turn
Feminism isn't one thing. The version adopted determines what's visible — and who gets protection.
Liberal feminism: "Add women and stir." Gender-sensitive asylum guidance, vulnerability categories, procedural adjustments. Operates within the existing architecture without questioning its racial, colonial, or class-based structure. This is what governments mean by "feminist" credentials on migration.
Nationalist feminism: Weaponizing gender to justify exclusion. Migrant men as threats to "our" women. Sexual violence narratives legitimizing border closure. This is what far-right parties do with gender.
Intersectional / decolonial feminism: The border regime itself is a feminist issue — structured by race, coloniality, class, citizenship (Achiume, Mohanty, Freedman, Krause, Crenshaw). The refugee definition historically protected the European intellectual man. Gendered human rights violations systemically under-recognised.
The analytical engine: Liberal feminism can't counter nationalist feminism because it doesn't address race and coloniality. The contest isn't progressive vs. regressive — it's between fundamentally different feminisms. The version adopted has material consequences for who moves and who's protected.

↓ case study: UK emerged as strongest practical and intellectual fit
09Case: UK — the privatised dispersal system as gendered implementation gapunder review
The UK asylum system is privatised, geographical, and gendered — and these three dimensions interact in ways that have barely been studied together. The dispersal system is the site where all six threads converge.
The implementation gap: The UK claims gender-sensitive asylum governance (Home Office Gender Issues guidance, Istanbul Convention, CEDAW). But women fleeing gender-based violence are placed on a no-choice basis by private contractors into accommodation that may be far from specialist legal services, women's organisations, and trauma-informed support. The gap between commitment and outcome is literal and measurable.
The privatised partnership chain: Three corporations — Serco, Mears, and Clearsprings Ready Homes — run the entire asylum accommodation system under AASC contracts worth billions. Clearsprings' contract value grew tenfold (£0.7bn to £7bn) since 2019. Hotels are more profitable than dispersal housing. The state outsources gender-sensitive obligations to companies whose profit incentives undermine those obligations. Below these sit subcontractors, private landlords, and hotel operators — each adding a layer of distance from the stated commitments.
The missing partnerships: The contract architecture connects the Home Office to corporate providers but is systematically disconnected from feminist civil society (Women for Refugee Women, Sisters Not Strangers, Rights of Women, Southall Black Sisters, local rape crisis centres). The partnerships that exist serve market logic; the partnerships that would serve gendered protection don't exist in the formal system.
The spatial dimension (GIS): Map where women asylum seekers are dispersed against where specialist gender-based violence services, legal aid providers, and women's organisations exist. The gap between placement and services is the implementation gap made geographically visible — and it's produced by the dispersal system's cost-driven logic.
The political context: Reform UK's gendered threat narratives push Labour toward deterrence and cost reduction, making gender sensitivity a low political priority. The securitisation environment shapes the conditions under which the partnership chain operates.
Not the undergrad: That asked how meso-level actors foster belonging (integration question, from below). This asks how a privatised governance system mediates the gap between gender-sensitive commitments and protection outcomes (implementation question, across scales, mixed methods with GIS).
Practical advantages: Asylum support data by local authority (publicly available, disaggregable). AASC contract details through NAO and parliamentary reports. Legal aid provider locations mappable. Full English-language sources. Potential interviews in London. Existing knowledge base. Feasible in under four months.

Part 5 — The emerging research question

Research question under review — see concerns below

How does the UK's privatised asylum dispersal system mediate the gap between gender-sensitive policy commitments and protection outcomes for women seeking asylum, and what do the spatial, political, and economic dynamics of this gap reveal about the limits of liberal feminist governance under conditions of securitisation?

Layer 1: The policy commitment (qualitative — policy analysis) — Map the UK's stated gender-sensitive framework: Home Office Gender Issues guidance, Istanbul Convention obligations, CEDAW commitments, AASC contract specifications for identifying vulnerable people. This is the "what the state says it does" layer.
Layer 2: The implementation gap — spatially mapped (mixed methods — GIS) — The centrepiece. Map where women asylum seekers are dispersed against where specialist GBV legal services, women's asylum organisations, and legal aid providers are located. Which contractor operates in each region, and what are their obligations? The GIS analysis makes the gap literal, measurable, and geographically visible.
Layer 3: The privatised partnership chain (qualitative — document analysis) — Analyse the AASC contract terms: what are Serco, Mears, and Clearsprings contractually required to do regarding gender? How do profit incentives (hotels more profitable than dispersal, no-choice placement, cost minimisation) shape outcomes? Why do partnerships with corporate providers exist but formal partnerships with feminist civil society organisations don't?
Layer 4: The political context (qualitative — contextual framing) — Reform UK's gendered threat narratives, Labour's deterrence response, and the securitisation environment that makes gender-sensitivity a low priority. Not a standalone analysis — the political wind that explains why the system is structured the way it is.
Conclusion: Constructive dimension — drawing on solidarity cities literature, intersectional migration scholarship, and examples of local authorities or NGOs that have built gender-responsive models from below, outline what genuinely feminist asylum governance would require.

Part 5b — The partnership chain: how gender degrades through outsourcing

The UK asylum system is a chain of outsourced partnerships. At every link, the gender-sensitive commitment degrades because of how the partnership is structured.

Link 1: Home Office → Serco, Mears, Clearsprings (the mega-contracts)

Three corporations deliver all asylum accommodation. Contracts worth billions (Clearsprings: £0.7bn → £7bn since 2019). Contract includes requirements to identify vulnerable people, but performance deductions amount to less than 1% of supplier revenue. No meaningful financial penalty for failing women.

Link 2: Contractors → subcontractors and private landlords

The three primary contractors don't own most accommodation. They subcontract to private landlords, hotel chains, and local property managers. Each layer adds corporate distance between the woman and the Home Office's gender commitments. Who is responsible for her safety?

Link 3: Home Office → Migrant Help (the AIRE contract)

A national helpline and support service, independent of providers. Supposed to be the safety net where asylum seekers report problems. Does it function as intended for women with gender-specific concerns, or is it a procedural box-tick?

Link 4: The absent partnerships — what doesn't exist

No formal partnership mechanisms connect the dispersal system to specialist women's organisations (Women for Refugee Women, Rights of Women, Southall Black Sisters, local rape crisis centres). These organisations provide the gender-sensitive support that actually matters — but they operate outside the contract architecture, funded precariously, often unable to reach women dispersed far away. The partnerships that exist serve market logic; the partnerships that would serve gendered protection don't exist.

Link 5: Local authorities → everyone

Councils aren't responsible for accommodation but bear the consequences. Some have built their own partnership models with local NGOs, health trusts, and community organisations to fill the gap the national system leaves. These vary enormously by geography, political orientation, and resources — and they're the solidarity-from-below examples for the constructive conclusion.

Part 5c — Unresolved concerns with the current framing

Honest problems identified

1. The gendered connection is unclear. The asylum dispersal system isn't primarily a gendered system — it's a cost-driven, geographical system that affects everyone regardless of sex. Attaching a gender lens to it requires assumptions that don't hold up under scrutiny. Gender is being imposed on the case rather than emerging naturally from it.

2. The assumption about GBV. The framing assumed that women in the asylum system are primarily GBV survivors who need specialist gender services. In reality, many women are dependants on family claims, or claiming on grounds unrelated to gender-based violence. The analysis jumped to conclusions about a homogeneous population that doesn't exist.

3. GIS mapping doesn't hold. Without sex-disaggregated dispersal data at the local authority level, mapping "where women are" against "where gender services exist" is impossible. Mapping total dispersal against services shows a general access-to-justice problem, not a gendered one. The spatial methodology was promising more than the data can deliver.

4. The research question may need rethinking from the ground up. These aren't minor refinements — they challenge the core logic connecting the empirical case (dispersal) to the analytical lens (gender). The decision is to step back and reconsider rather than patch.

Part 5e — Possible alternative directions to explore

Direction A: Immigration detention of women. Gender is built into this case by design — women-specific facilities (Yarl's Wood, Derwentside), run by Serco on multi-million-pound contracts. 85% of women detained are GBV survivors. 89% are released (shouldn't have been detained). HMIP inspection reports are gendered, detailed, and public. The corporate dimension is concentrated and traceable. Derwentside was converted from female to male in 2024 to serve deterrence goals — the political dimension is visible. Strongest option if gender remains central.

Direction B: The privatised asylum system without forcing gender. Study the dispersal/accommodation system as a site of negotiation between humanitarian obligations, corporate profit, and securitisation — without making gender the central lens. This plays to your capitalism + nationalism interests. Gender could be one dimension rather than the organising framework.

Direction C: A different empirical case entirely that holds the six threads together. This would require a conversation with your supervisor to identify — the brainstorming process with an AI may have reached its limits.

On scope:

How do I prevent stretching across too many dimensions (gender + nationalism + capitalism + solidarity + spatial analysis)? What's the disciplinary boundary — is this feminist IR, critical migration studies, political economy of asylum, or something else? And does that matter for assessment?

On the "which feminism" question:

The most analytically sharp insight from this process was that different feminisms (liberal, nationalist, intersectional) structure governance differently. Is there an empirical case where this can be studied without the data problems identified above? Could this become the theoretical framework rather than the empirical focus?

On the constructive dimension:

I want the dissertation to point toward alternatives, not just critique. How much space should this take? Is it a concluding remark, a full chapter, or something in between? Can solidarity cities or feminist civil society practices be the constructive anchor?
